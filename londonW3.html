AS A SMART SUSTAINABLE CITY



As the Internet of Things (IoT) market is continuously growing, the potential for smart city development has to be considered. Furthermore, protecting the environment is one of the most important challenges nowadays, thus a lot of consideration has to be put into maximizing the sustainability of a city. This is report seizing London’s potential for smart sustainable development. The piece is divided under three main sectors: smart waste, smart energy and smart water. The analysis is done based on the government’s initiative, as well as projects developed by major companies that seek innovation within the current system. Potential developments and issues are discussed that could eventually be integrated in a sustainability dashboard that manages the whole ecosystem.

Smart Waste.

For the past seven years, the City of London has developed a plan to ensure London’s permanent care and growth of the system that is taking care of managing the waste, under the initiative “The city of London Waste Strategy 2013-2020”. In 2011 the Mayor creates a strategy, under the name “London’s Wasted Resource”, where the subsequent goals are followed:

■ Achieve zero municipal waste direct to landfill by 2025

■ Reduce the amount of household waste produced in 2008/09 from 970kg to 790kg per household by 2031

■ Increase London’s capacity to reuse or repair municipal waste from approximately 6,000 tonnes per annum in 2008 to 40,000 in 2012 and 120,000 in 2031

■ Recycle or compost at least 45% of municipal waste by 2015, 50% by 2020 and 60% 2031

■ The management of London’s municipal waste to achieve annual greenhouse gas emissions savings of approximately: 1.2 million tonnes of CO2eq in 2015 1.4 million tonnes of CO2eq in 2020 1.6 million tonnes of CO2eq in 2031

■ To generate as much energy as possible from London’s organic and non-recyclable waste in a way that is no more polluting in carbon terms than the energy source it is replacing. This is estimated to be possible for about 40% of London’s municipal waste after recycling or composting targets are achieved by 2031

Credits: The Mayor's Municipial Waste Management Strategy

Another strategy undertaken by the Mayor underlines the importance of managing the municipal waste locally in order to act accordingly to the maximum net self-sufficiency by 2031 goal disclosed in The London Plan. The latter strategy consists of six planning policies that involve waste issues across all London:

■Policy One: Informing producers and consumers of the value of reducing, reusing and recycling municipal waste

■ Policy Two: Reducing the climate change impact of London’s municipal waste management

■ Policy Three: Capturing the economic benefits of municipal waste management

■ Policy Four: Achieving high recycling or composting rates resulting in the greatest environmental and financial benefits

■ Policy Five: Stimulating the development of new municipal waste management infrastructure, particularly low carbon technologies

■ Policy Six: Achieving a high level of street cleanliness

Credits: The Mayor's Municipial Waste Management Strategy

Smart Energy

According to the Arups’s report “Smart City Opportunities for London” smart energy management technologies are defined as the following:

“technologies [that] can help utilities and distributors to forecast and manage loads better, reduce the need for costly infrastructure expansion, and improve service quality and customer satisfaction. Meanwhile consumers benefit from service quality reliability improvements, new tariff options, the ability to reduce their energy bills.”

BIS Report

The United Kingdom government sector in charge of energy management is The Department for Energy and Climate Change (DECG). Therefore, any new initiative for energy supplying must be agreed within the Energy Technology List (ETL). Such new technology proposals might include:

■ Automatic power down for IT equipment

■ Corrosion prevention / treatment systems

■ De-areators for wet heating systems

■ Electricity generators / turbines / solar cells

■ Insulating curtains/doors for cold rooms

■ Kettles and point of use water heaters

■ Low loss valves for steam systems etc.

■ Lubricants, fuel additives, novel fuels etc.

■ Mechanical clutches, gearboxes, seals etc.

■ Remote controls for electrical equipment

■ Software for analysing energy/carbon use

■ Water treatment plant (legionella control)

How is climate change affecting London?

As we all know the climate is constantly changing. As a consequence, according to the UK government, the summers became hotter and drier, while the winters became warmer and wetter. These results are believed to be caused by people’s activities, which ultimately lead to emission of greenhouse gases (i.e. carbon dioxide).

The government’s response to this issue involves reducing the city’s greenhouse gas emissions in order to stop additional developing, as well as adjusting to the new climate conditions.

Firstly, in order to tackle the latter issue the Mayor set the goal for London to achieve a zero level of carbon by 2050. As found in the governmental research, 80% of the gas emissions are a result of powering and heating buildings by burning fossil fuels, while the remaining part results from transport. More information related to the Mayor’s initiative, London Environment Strategy, to solve this problem involves:

■ our energy efficiency programmes are cutting London’s energy use from buildings

■ our energy supply programmes are working to supply more of London’s energy locally so it is more secure, affordable and low carbon

■ we’re supporting the use of low carbon hydrogen to power transport and heat and power buildings

■ we’re working with Transport for London to cut energy use from transport and use new technologies to cut emission

■ we’re measuring the amount of greenhouses gases in London every year through the London Energy and Greenhouse Gas Inventory

■ we’re also increasing jobs and investment into our climate change programmes through our green economy work

Credits: Gov.uk

Secondly, the Mayor’s plan for adapting to the extreme climate changes that were previosly mentioned, involves taking into consideration the high risk sitations, such as floods, droughts and heat. The latter weather conditions are belived to be become more frequent and serious, if we do not step in. Therefore, in the Mayor’s plan for climate change, Managing Risks and Increasing Resilience, the following steps are explored:

■ helping to reduce flood risk in London through our Drain London programme

■ helping London to cope with droughts through our water supply programmes

■ supporting more green roofs, green walls and local green spaces to help London cope with overheating, flooding and drought

■ helping to reduce heat risk

Credits:Gov.uk


Siemens Smart Grids: driving energy intelligence


Siemens initiative involves integrating smart energy within the energy supply as a more effective, secure and adaptable source. However, renewable supplies can be harder to integrate in the current structure. Therefore, Siemens proposes decentralized power generation and intelligent storage in order to permit society to control their electricity. Moreover, due to the growing usage of renewable energy power, the respective systems must be able of “managing multi-directional power flows, highly volatile power generation, and large variations in demand”. Thus, a smart infrastructure that can adapt the growing complexity of the model is sought.

“Smart grids are shaping opportunities for a new energy age: They automatically balance demand and generation, handle multi-directional power flows and ensure the energy transition to seamlessly incorporate photovoltaics and other renewables, eMobility charging and energy storage into the energy ecosystem.”

-Siemens


Smart Water.

London’s water systems are mainly managed by four companies: Affinity Water, Thames Water, Essex and Suffolk Water, and Sutton and East Surrey Water. However, according to Arup’s study, Thames Water is responsible for approximately 80% of the city’s market.

According to Thames Water, they established the largest and most effective water business in the United Kingdom. As a consequence, the programme involves collecting everyday 34 million litres of water in the 2015-2020 timeframe. In addition to this, Thames Water is involved in the Love Water Campaign. The latter aims at raising awareness regarding current situation, as well as encouraging people to get involved in protecting it. In the video bellow Thames Water's future development for the following five years is described, which includes a series of adjustments to improve sustainability.

The United Kingdom Government is responding to this issue within The Department for International Trade (DIT). The latter in responsible for a variety of sources, resulting in the following: products, technology and consultancy. Therefore, the competence is achieved by the subsequent principles:

■ water sourcing

■ quality water delivery

■ treated water for reuse

■ balancing water usage

■ digital utility

■ flood resilience

■ advisory services

■ research, innovation and training

Credits:Gov.uk

Under the digital utility section, it is argued that currently there are a range of companies based in the United Kingdom that are working on the revolution of water systems by collecting data. Therefore, as stated, by using “leading-edge modelling and analytics tools”, the companies can enhance the overall customer experience by adapting “asset and operational data into actionable insights”. As a consequence, the automation appliances can lead to a decrease of operational costs, while busting accuracy and effectiveness at their full potential.

How can analytic tools maximise efficiency?

Analytic tools can allow water companies to imagine a variety of scenarios from user experience to business models. Thus, they can quickly apply it to the situation and enhance the analysis process. As stated, the latter tools can be applied to operational and investment enterprises, resulting in an innovative manner of controlling how water is handled. Taking into consideration all the previous statements, applications that handle data, consist of the following:

■ water resources planning – simulating resource system behaviour, maximising yield, safeguarding supplies and minimising cost

■ water network pump scheduling – minimising operating costs by optimising pump operation in relation to energy tariff rates, demand forecasts and reservoir profiles, using real-time information

■ water and wastewater network control – providing control-room capability to optimise network performance using real-time information

■ capital and operational intervention planning – identifying optimal combined investment and operational intervention strategies to achieve defined serviceability and cost targets

Credits:Gov.uk

Conclusion

To conclude London has the potential for a huge development within the smart city area. Nevertheless, sustainability has to be considered at all stages. As the market is rapidly growing and industries are developing more projects, innovative and exciting changes will be seen in the future. With the implementation of a smart city dashboard, people will gain access to the information, enabling them to be part of the ecosystem. However, we all must proceed carefully while adjusting and fitting all needs.
